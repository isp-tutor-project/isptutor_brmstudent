State Change: Evaporation and Melting

What is Evaporation? Say you are heating a pot of water on the stove (shown below). Thermal energy is produced in the chemical reaction of burning natural gas (which is mostly methane). This reaction produces high-energy (or very fast-moving!) CO2 and H2O molecules. These molecules hit the metal molecules that make up the pot. As a result, the molecules of the pot start vibrating faster. Thermal energy is then transferred between the pot's molecules and from the pot's molecules to nearby water molecules. This causes the water molecules to move around faster and faster. The water's temperature is increasing.



Soon, some water molecules move fast enough to overcome the pull from electric forces of other water molecules around them. They also have to overcome the electric forces of the water molecules at the surface. When they are moving fast enough, the water molecules leave the pot and move into the air. This is what we know as evaporation. 

When a liquid like water evaporates, the molecules themselves do not change when they move from the pot into the air. They are still H2O molecules (two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom); this is a physical reaction.
 
Q1: Which of the following statements do you think is true?
a) Water evaporates faster when it is at a higher temperature.

b) Water evaporates faster when it is at a lower temperature.

c) Water evaporates equally fast at higher and lower temperatures.

d) There is no way to predict this relationship.

Right! If water is at a higher temperature, it will evaporate faster.
Actually, if water is at a higher temperature, it will evaporate faster.
Q1.5: Explain in your own words why water evaporates faster when it is at a higher temperature.
If water is at a higher temperature, the molecules that make up the water are moving around faster. So, they have more energy to break their bonds with other H2O molecules and escape into the air (or evaporate). This is why water evaporates faster at a higher temperature.

Analogies for Evaporation. Evaporation is similar to a rocket blasting off and escaping from the Earth's gravitational field. Rockets need to have enough speed to overcome the pull of gravity and escape from the Earth's atmosphere. To escape into the air (or evaporate), water molecules need to have enough speed to overcome the electric forces of other H2O molecules within the liquid water and especially at the surface of the water (where H2O molecules are even more strongly bonded together).



Another analogy for evaporation is rolling a magnetic steel ball on a table past a magnet. If you roll the ball slowly, the ball will get "caught" in the magnetic field and "stick" to the magnet. But if you roll the ball faster, it will have enough speed to overcome the magnetic attraction and move past the magnet. This is why H2O molecules that are moving around faster are more likely to evaporate. They can "escape" from the electric forces of the other H2O molecules.

Evaporation of salt water. In the unit on dissolving, we talked about how salt (and also sugar) dissolve in water. But what happens when salt water evaporates? Let's say we have a glass of salt water sitting on a window sill. We forget about it and discover it several weeks later. What will we find?

Most likely, most (if not all) of the water will have evaporated. This happens because eventually, individual water molecules will gain enough kinetic energy (from the sun or surrounding air) to evaporate. But the originally dissolved salt (NaCl) will not evaporate with the water molecules. Why not?

One reason that the NaCl molecules will not evaporate even though H2O molecules do is because they are much heavier than H2O molecules. If we look at the Periodic Table of Elements, we see that the atomic mass of Na is 23 "atomic mass units" or amu. And Cl is about 35 amu. 

  • So, one molecules of NaCl is 23 amu + 35 amu = 58 amu.

    • In H2O, there are two H atoms. Each H atom is only 1 amu. There is one O atom, which is 16 amu.

  • So, one H2O molecule is only 18 amu.

    • Because NaCl molecules are heavier, they are pulled downward by the Earth's gravity more strongly than H2O molecules. So, they do not escape from the glass and evaporate into the air.     What is Melting? When thermal energy is transferred to ice, the H2O molecules begin to vibrate faster and faster. Some of the molecules have enough energy to overcome their bonds with nearby H2O molecules. These molecules are no longer fixed in place in the ice crystal (see Crystals unit) and can freely move about. These molecules are now in liquid form. If enough thermal energy is transferred to the ice, the ice will melt completely and become liquid water. The H2O molecules are now moving around independently, kind of "slithering" past each other. But there are still attractions between H2O molecules because of electric forces (see the Atoms Unit for more information).

    Important things to understand (that many people don't):
    • When H2O changes from solid to liquid to gas is that the individual H2O molecules stay together. They do NOT break up into individual atoms. This idea is true for any type of molecule that changes state: the molecule stays the same!
    • Another important point is that each individual molecule stays the same size during state change. The molecules themselves do not get bigger or smaller; they just move around more or less. (Some people mistakenly think that molecules themselves get bigger or smaller as an object gets warmer or cooler. But they don't! The molecules stay the same size! Molecules just move around more —and take up more space when they are moving around more/faster. And molecules move around less—and take up less space—when they are moving around more slowly. It's kind of like when people are dancing: they take up more space when they are moving around more (and bumping into other people!). But of course, people stay the same size.

    Q2: Briefly explain why an ice cube in a 26 degree C room will melt faster than the same ice cube in a 20 degree C room. (Hint: Think about what happens when molecules of air collide with H2O molecules of the ice cube.)
    The ice will melt faster in the warmer room because the air molecules are moving around faster, on average, in the warmer room. So, when the air molecules collide with the H2O molecules in the ice cube, there is a greater chance that the H2O molecules will gain enough speed to escape from the electric forces holding the H2O molecules in the crystal ice.

    Q3: Which is true about melting and evaporation?
    a) They are both chemical processes.

    b) They are both physical processes.

    c) Melting is chemical and evaporation is physical.

    d) Melting is physical and evaporation is chemical.

    Right! Since the molecules stay the same when they evaporate or melt, both processes are physical.
    Actually, since the molecules stay the same when they evaporate or melt, both processes are physical.
    Q4: What happens to H2O molecules of ice as the temperature of the ice increases from -20 degrees C to 0 degrees C.
    a) Nothing. The H2O molecules are in a solid state (ice) in this temperature range. They don't move.

    b) Nothing. The H2O molecules move around a little (and the same amount) in this temperature range.

    c) The H2O molecules will move around faster as the temperature increases from -20 to 0 degrees C.

    d) The H2O molecules will move around slower as the temperature increases from -20 to 0 degrees C.

    Right! The H2O molecules will slow as the temperature increases from -20 to 0 degrees C. (c) is the correct answer.
    Actually, the H2O molecules will slow as the temperature increases from -20 to 0 degrees C. (c) is the correct answer.
    Q5: Briefly explain why an ice cube that is -2 degree C would take less time to melt in the same room (at 20 degree C) than an ice cube that is -10 degree C. Hint: Think about the collisions between air molecules and H2O molecules.
    The H2O molecules in the higher-temperature ice cube will be vibrating faster in the crystal ice structure. So, they do not need as much extra "push" from surrounding air molecules that collide with them to allow them to escape from the electric forces due to other H2O molecules in the ice crystal.
