Electric Force

Electric force.  Oppositely-charged atomic particles (protons and electrons) attract each other, and similarly-charged particles repel (or push each other away). We call these forces between charged particles "electric forces." 

The arrows in the pictures below show the direction of the electric force on particles caused by nearby charged particles.


The electric force is what causes electrons and protons to attract each other. So, the electric force causes oppositely charged particles to attract each other.
  • Electrons orbit around the nucleus-rather than flying off away from the atoms-because of the attractive electric force between electrons and protons. In other words, the protons "pull" the electrons toward them, keeping the electrons orbiting around the nucleus.
  • This is kind of like our solar system, where the sun is the nucleus in the center, and the planets are like the electrons. Just like the pull from gravity causes the planets to orbit around the sun, the electric force's pull between oppositely-charged particles causes the electrons to orbit around the nucleus.
The electric force also causes electrons to repel (or push away) other electrons and protons to repel other protons. So the electric force also causes particles with the same charge to repel each other.
 

Everyday example of electric force.  You almost certainly have experienced electric forces personally. For example, if you rub a balloon on your hair, your hair will "stick" to the balloon (see the picture below). This is because when the balloon material rubbed your hair, electrons from your hair moved to the balloon.  So, now the balloon is negatively charged (represented as red negative signs in the picture on the right). Your hair is positively charged (represented as positive signs). So your hair will be attracted to the balloon through the electric force.
