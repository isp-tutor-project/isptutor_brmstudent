About this website (for teachers). This website is part of the ISP (Inquiry-based Science Project) Tutor, for middle school students. It was developed under a research grant from the Department of Education to Prof. David Klahr (PI) and Dr. Stephanie Siler (Co-PI) and a team of middle school teachers and education researchers at Carnegie Mellon University. The content of this website is based on Next Generation Science Standards and designed to support students' background research for common science fair project research questions (e.g., the research question shown above).

Below is a lesson on doing background research for a chosen research question. This lesson may be split into individual lessons on selecting good research questions (Steps 1 & 2), planning research (Steps 2 - 4), or given in its entirety (Steps 1 - 6). Students can download an editable booklet that gives directions and asks questions throughout the lesson. Students then view a video of a virtual student deciding on a research topic by considering what they already know (Step 1). Next, students work through a lesson in which they select a research topic and question (Step 2). Then, students can view a brief lesson where a virtual student explains how they plan for their background research (Step 3). In Step 4, students plan their own research. Afterward, in Step 5, students watch a video in which a virtual student conducts background research and synthesizes information to form a hypothesis for their research question (research has shown students have difficulty with this). Finally, students use this website to do background research for their research question and ultimately develop a hypothesis for their research question (Step 6).

Note for teachers: You and your students can also use this website as a resource to find various middle school level science units. You can do this by typing search terms into the search box above. Or you can click here to see a list of all units in this website.

BACKGROUND RESEARCH LESSON 


Students: First, download a booklet for this lesson here. Follow the directions on this booklet for this lesson.


STEP 1: First, you will watch a lesson on selecting a good research question here. (There is audio, so you may need headphones.)

Answer the questions for STEP 1 in your booklet.


STEP 2: Now, select your research question here. (Click on the blank page to start.) This lesson also has audio, so you may need headphones. Try to apply the methods used by the student in the lesson you watched when picking your research question.

Answer the questions for STEP 2 in your booklet.




STEP 3: HELP IN PLANNING YOUR RESEARCH 

For important tips on doing background research, click on the Plan Your Research video button below. Take notes in your booklet.


STEP 4: PLAN YOUR RESEARCH 

Now, think about what information you need to research to answer your research question. Click on your experimental topic below to watch (or re-watch) an animation for one trial of your experiment to get more ideas about what information to look up.

Crystal Growth; Soda/Mint; Ice Melting; Gas in Balloon; Inclined Plane; Objects Sinking; Greenhouse; Algae


Write down the information you want to look up in your booklet (pages 4-5).




STEP 5: HELP IN DOING BACKGROUND RESEARCH...

Background Research lesson: Now, click here to watch a lesson in which a virtual "high school student" is doing background research for their research question. (This video has sound.)

Take notes of this lesson in your booklet.



HOW TO USE THIS WEBSITE TO FIND INFORMATION...

You can find science units relevant to your research question by clicking on a blue button above for the science area of your research question (e.g., "Forces & Motion"). You'll see a list of units relevant to the topic you chose.

Or, you can type what you would like to find out about in the search bar above (as shown below). Click on the little magnifying glass and you'll see a list of units that include those key words (you may see some Ads in the list of results: just ignore those). Just click on the link of a unit you'd like to look at.



You can also type "glossary" in the search bar to get a list of basic definitions of science concepts.


When you're in a unit, you may need to click on the orange arrows at the bottom of the page to find the info you're looking for (since many units have more than one page).

You can always come back to this page by clicking on the "Home & HELP" button at the top of the page you're on (the Help and four Areas of Science buttons will be on every page).

If you still need help and don't know what to do, ask your teacher (or another adult) to help you.



STEP 6: NOW, DO YOUR BACKGROUND RESEARCH!!

Follow the instructions starting on page 7 of your booklet, which will help you to find information for your research question.
