Kinetic Energy and Temperature

Kinetic Energy and Temperature. All moving objects (including atoms and molecules) have a form of energy called kinetic energy. This is the energy that objects have that is due to their movement. Because there are different types of motion, there are different kinds of kinetic energy. Two types of kinetic energy are (a) rotational kinetic energy and (b) translational kinetic energy. In this section, we will talk about the translational kinetic energy of an object. This is the kinetic energy of objects due to their movement over a distance. 

Translational kinetic energy is typically the type of kinetic energy people mean when they talk about "kinetic energy." Translational kinetic energy is also the type of kinetic energy that defines an object's temperature. In this section, we will be talking about translational kinetic energy, but we will refer to it as simply "kinetic energy" here.

The relationship between the kinetic energy of an object and its mass (m) and speed (or velocity, v) is shown below:


  • For objects moving at the same speed, more massive objects have greater kinetic energy than less massive objects. 
  • For objects of the same mass, faster-moving objects have greater kinetic energy than slower-moving objects.
The temperature of an object is the average kinetic energy of its atoms and/or molecules.

Q1: What is the kinetic energy of an object that is not moving?
(zero) Because KE = 1/2mv2, if v is zero, the KE of the object would also be zero.


Q2: Based on the formula for kinetic energy, does an object's mass or speed/velocity have a greater effect on an object's kinetic energy?
(The speed/velocity) Because KE = 1/2mv2, changes in speed/velocity would have a greater effect on the object's KE than the object's mass. (BUT: only true above 1!)


Q3: A Hydrogen molecule (H2) has a mass of 2 amu. (amu stands for "atomic mass units", which is equal to the mass of one proton or neutron, which is 0.00000000000000000000000334 grams.) This molecule is moving around in the Earth's atmosphere at a speed of 0.525 km/s. What is its kinetic energy?
a) 0.525 amu × km/s

b) 1.050 amu × km/s

c) 0.276 amu × km2/s2

d) None of the above.

Right!
KE = 1/2*mass*velocity^2 = 1/2*(2 amu)(0.525)^2 = 0.276 amu*m^2/s^2
Well, actually.....

Temperature increases as kinetic energy of atoms/molecules increases. The temperature of an object is a measure of the average (translational!) kinetic energy (or energy of motion) of the particles (atoms and/or molecules) that make up that object. The kinetic energy of individual particles is determined by both their speed and mass. 

Let's say there are two objects made of the same types of particles (e.g., aluminum atoms). As shown in the picture below (a microscopic view), the aluminum atoms of Object 1 (below, left) are moving at about twice the average speed of the aluminum atoms of Object 2 (below, right). The length of the arrows represents the speed of the atoms: longer arrows mean higher speed. The direction of the arrows shows the direction of motion at that time point.

                                 
Note: Because these atoms make up a solid object, they do not move freely around, but rather vibrate in a fixed location.

  • Because its atoms are moving faster, we know that the temperature of Object 1 is higher than the temperature of Object 2.

Temperature increases as the mass of atoms/molecules increases. The temperature of an object also depends on the mass of the atoms and/or molecules that make up that object. 

Now let's say we have an aluminum object and a silver object. The aluminum atoms of Object 1 (shown in the picture below, left) and the silver atoms of Object 3 (shown below, right) have the same average speed.


  • A silver atom has more mass than an aluminum atom (see the Periodic table, which includes the "atomic mass" for each element). So, the atoms of the silver object (Object 3 above) have more average kinetic energy than the atoms of the aluminum object (Object 1 above). In other words, the silver object has a higher temperature than the aluminum object.


Q4: There are two glasses of water (H2O). You measure the temperature of the water in Glass 1 as 15 degrees C. You measure the temperature of the water in Glass 2 as 30 degrees C. Which of the following is true:
a) On average, the H2O molecules in Glass 1 are moving around faster than H2O molecules in Glass 2.

b) On average, the H2O molecules in Glass 2 are moving around faster than H2O molecules in Glass 1.

c) On average, the H2O in Glass 1 are moving around at the same speed as H2O in Glass 2.

d) More information is needed to answer.

You're right! The temperature of the water in Glass 2 is higher than in Glass 1. We know that the temperature of a substance is calculated as the average kinetic energy (1/2mv2) of the atoms/molecules of the substance (in this case, water). All water molecules (H2O) have the same mass. So, the molecules of water in Glass 2 must have a higher speed/velocity than water molecules in Glass 1.

Q5: A gold ring and a glass of water that are sitting on a desk are at the same temperature (room temperature). What do you think is true of the average speed of the ring's gold atoms vs. the water molecules? (Hint: look at the periodic table)
a) They are the same

b) The gold atoms move faster on average

c) The water molecules move faster on average

d) I don't know.

The temperature of a substance (e.g., glass of water) is its AVERAGE kinetic energy. So, the average KE of the water is the same as the average KE of the gold. Because gold atoms are more massive than water molecules, the gold atoms will have lower speed/velocity in general.
