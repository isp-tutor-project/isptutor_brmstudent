Algae

Algae are defined as simple nonflowering plants. They are part of a large group that includes multi-cellular forms (such as seaweeds) and many single-celled forms. Algae contain chlorophyll, so they are autotropic (from Greek, “auto” meaning “self”, and “troph” meaning “nourishing”). This means they can make their own food through photosynthesis. This means they need light to make their own food and to grow. See the Photosynthesis  unit for a graph of the relationship between light and growth of algae.

However, algae lack true stems, roots, leaves, and vascular tissue. Thus, algae are classified as non-vascular plants (lacking in tubes to transport water throughout the plant).

The picture above is an example of a multi-cellular form of algae (brown algae). Even though it resembles many vascular plants, it does not have tubes to transport water throughout the plant or roots that absorb water and nutrients (and transport them throughout the plant). The function of the root-like structure is to hold the plant in place. (Appropriately, the root-like structures are called "holdfasts.") 

As you can probably easily imagine, single-celled algae also lack stems, roots, leaves, and vascular tissues.


How big are algae?

Algae range in size from microscopic, single cells to multi-cell plants many meters in length.

  • Algae are plants with at least one cell; each cell contains a nucleus with DNA.
  • One group of single-cell algae are “diatoms”. One-celled algae such as diatoms typically live together in colonies.
  • Seaweed are large, multi-cellular algae.

Where are algae found?

Algae can be found in most habitats on Earth. However, the majority are found in either freshwater environments (for example, lakes or ponds) or marine environments (i.e., in oceans, seas, bays, or other waterways and nearby land surfaces).

Algae can also live in soil, on the bark of trees, and many other places that are moist. The algae that live on the surface of ponds, lakes and oceans are an important food source. 

They are also responsible for the majority of oxygen in the Earth’s atmosphere! So, algae are very important plants for the Earth’s environment and its animals (including people).


Types of Algae 

Diatoms are a Class of uni-cellular (single-celled) algae. There are over 8,000 known species of diatoms, the most of any Class of algae. 

One species of diatoms is Chaetoceros muelleri. 

As you can see in the picture below, diatoms have glasslike cell walls. They are found on the surface of both fresh and salt water. Like all algae, diatoms use photosynthesis to convert light energy into food. 

Some species of diatoms are able to move by oozing chemicals through the slits of the cell walls and sliding on it. When diatoms die, their remains sink to the bottom of the water and form diatomaceous earth. This material is used in the manufacturing of toothpaste, and scouring products. 


The picture above shows various species of diatoms, one Class of algae.

Green algae (which includes the Phylum: Chlorophyta, which has about 4,500 known species) are found on land around the bases of trees in moist areas. They are also found in both fresh and salt water (near the surface). 

This form of algae can be either uni-cellular or multi-cellular. Seaweed (below, left) is one example of (multi-cellular) green algae. The algae is green due to the chlorophyll pigments it contains (like most plants). Green algae is also commonly found in swimming pools and fish tanks (see the picture below for a microscopic image of one type of green algae).

One type of Green Algae is shown below.




Red algae is another type of algae. There are over 7,000 species of Red Algae. Red algae tend to live in deeper waters. The pigment found within red algae is able to collect or absorb small amounts of light energy—primarily the higher-energy blue light that is able to travel farther through water than lower-energy light—and use this light in photosynthesis. This type of algae appears red because it reflects (lower-energy) red light. However, some species of “red” algae actually appear other colors, such as orange, yellow, or pink (but not blue).

Examples of red algae are multi-cellular seaweed (as shown below). Products from this material are used for products ranging from food including products to make iced cream to hair conditioners.




Brown Algae is a type of multi-cellular algae that includes over 1,000 species. One type of brown algae is what some people may think of as seaweed. Despite its name, brown algae contains pigments (called fucoxanthin) that reflect yellow light; as a result, brown algae appears yellow-ish (see the picture below). Energy from the (non-yellow) wavelengths of light that these (fucoxanthin) pigments absorb helps photosynthesis occur.


Brown algae—like that shown in the picture above—lives and thrive in cool, rocky waters, like off of the Atlantic coast of North America. Giant kelp—one type of brown algae—lives off the coast of the Pacific Ocean. Harvested brown algae is used in the production of pudding and salad dressing. 


Problems with algae over growth  

When there is an increase in the amount of nutrients in the water, algae may grow quickly. This can cause the water to turn in color to various shades such as brown, red, green and even become colorless, depending on the species of algae in “bloom”. These tides can become dangerous, because they are toxic or poisonous when algae reaches a high concentration. The toxins collect in the bodies of the organisms (for example, small fish) consuming or eating the algae, which may kill them. If these organisms (for example, smaller fish) do not die from eating the concentrated algae, predators (for example, larger fish) who eat them may become ill. 

Blooms can be found in both salt water and fresh water. Blooms in salt water are far more difficult to control because they have vast boundaries. However, humans are more likely to cause blooms in fresh water (than salt water) bodies.

The increase in nutrient concentration in fresh water bodies may be caused by the run-off of water from nearby land that has been fertilized. That is, when we over-fertilize our soil, the extra chemicals can dissolve in drainage water and seep into nearby bodies of water. The excessive buildup of nutrients in bodies of water in turn causes the over growth of algae. (This process is called eutrophication (from Greek, "eu" is  "good," and, remember that "troph" means "nourishing." But in this case, the algae are getting too much nourishment!)
