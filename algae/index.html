Algae

Algae are defined as simple plants that do not have flowers. There are types of algae that have many cells (such as seaweeds) and other types of algae that are only made of one single cell (like "diatoms"). Algae contain chlorophyll, so they are autotropic (from Greek, “auto” meaning “self”, and “troph” meaning “nourishing”). This means they make their own food using light in the process of photosynthesis.  

Algae lack true stems, roots, leaves, and vascular tissue. So, algae are non-vascular plants, which means they don't have tubes to transport water throughout the plant.

The picture above is an example of a multi-cellular (many-celled) form of algae, brown algae. Even though it looks like many vascular plants, it doesn't have tubes to transport water throughout the plant or roots that absorb water and nutrients (and transport them throughout the plant). The function of the root-like structure--called "holdfasts" (makes sense, right?)--is to hold the plant in place. Another important part of brown algae are the "gas bladders." Though they might look kind of like flowers (or fruits), they are not flowers. The gas bladders contain (you guessed it!) gases, which help keep the algae from sinking to the bottom of the ocean, where they would die due to lack of sunlight.

As you may have guessed, single-celled algae don't have all of these parts: stems, roots, leaves, and vascular tissues. (All of these parts are made of lots and lots of cells!)


How big are algae?

Algae range in size from microscopic, single cells, to multi-cellular plants many meters in length.

  • Algae are plants made of at least one cell; each cell contains a nucleus with DNA.
  • One group of single-cell algae are “diatoms”. One-celled algae such as diatoms typically live together in colonies.
  • Seaweed are large, multi-cellular algae.

Where are algae found?

Algae can be found in most habitats on Earth. However, the majority are found in either freshwater environments (for example, lakes or ponds) or marine environments (i.e., in oceans, seas, bays, or other waterways and nearby land surfaces).

Algae can also live in soil, on the bark of trees, and many other places that are moist. The algae that live on the surface of ponds, lakes and oceans are an important food source. 

They are also responsible for the majority of oxygen in the Earth’s atmosphere! So, algae are very important plants for the Earth’s environment and its animals (including people).

Types of Algae 

Algae are extremely diverse: there are at least 30,000 different species of algae. The different species of algae respond differently to factors like light intensity/brightness, temperature, and the amount of carbon dioxide (CO2) dissolved in the water surrounding the algae.

Diatoms are one Class of uni-cellular (single-celled) algae. There are over 8,000 known species of diatoms, the most of any Class of algae. 

One species of diatoms is Chaetoceros muelleri. 

As shown in the picture below, diatoms have transparent cell walls that you can see through. They are found on surfaces of both fresh water (like lakes) and salt water (oceans). Like all algae, diatoms use photosynthesis to make their food. 

When diatoms die, their remains sink to the bottom of the water and form a layer. Shells of diatoms are a little bit rough. Because of this, people use it to make toothpaste and other cleaning products. 


The picture above shows various species of diatoms, one Class of algae.

Green algae (which includes the Phylum: Chlorophyta, which has about 4,500 known species) are found on land around the bases of trees in moist areas. They are also found in both fresh and salt water (near the surface). 

This form of algae can be either uni-cellular or multi-cellular. Seaweed (shown below) is one example of (multi-cellular) green algae. The algae is green due to the chlorophyll pigments it contains (like most plants). Green algae is also commonly found in swimming pools and fish tanks (see the picture below for a microscopic image of one type of green algae).

Red algae is another type of algae. There are over 7,000 species of Red Algae. Red algae tend to live in deeper waters. The pigment found within red algae is able to collect or absorb small amounts of light energy—mostly the higher-energy blue light that is able to travel farther through water than lower-energy light. This higher-energy blue light is used in photosynthesis. This type of algae appears red because it reflects (lower-energy) red light. But some species of “red” algae actually appear other colors, including orange, yellow, and pink (but not blue).

Examples of red algae are multi-cellular seaweed (as shown below). Products made from red algae range from food (such as ice cream) to hair conditioners.




Brown Algae is a type of multi-cellular algae that includes over 1,000 species. One type of brown algae is what some people may think of as seaweed. Despite its name, brown algae contains pigments (called fucoxanthin) that reflect yellow light; as a result, brown algae appears yellow-ish (see the picture below). Energy from the (non-yellow) wavelengths of light that these (fucoxanthin) pigments absorb helps photosynthesis occur.


Brown algae—like that shown in the picture above—lives and thrive in cool, rocky waters, like off of the Atlantic coast of North America. Giant kelp—one type of brown algae—lives off the coast of the Pacific Ocean. Harvested brown algae is used to make pudding and salad dressing. 


Problems with algae overgrowth  

When there is an increase in the amount of nutrients in the water (including CO2), algae may grow quickly. This can cause the water to turn in color to various shades such as brown, red, green and even become colorless, depending on the species of algae in “bloom”. These tides can become dangerous, because they are toxic or poisonous when algae reaches a high concentration. The toxins collect in the bodies of the organisms ( for example, small fish) consuming or eating the algae, which may kill them. If these organisms ( for example, smaller fish) do not die from eating the concentrated algae, predators (for example, larger fish) who eat them may become ill. 

Blooms can be found in both salt water and fresh water. Blooms in salt water are far more difficult to control because they have vast boundaries. However, humans are more likely to cause blooms in fresh water (than salt water) bodies.

The increase in nutrient concentration in fresh water bodies may be caused by the run-off of water from nearby land that has been fertilized. That is, when we over-fertilize our soil, the extra chemicals can dissolve in drainage water and seep into nearby bodies of water. The excessive buildup of nutrients in bodies of water in turn causes the over growth of algae. (This process is called eutrophication (from Greek, "eu" is  "good," and, remember that "troph" means "nourishing." But in this case, the algae are getting too much nourishment!)
